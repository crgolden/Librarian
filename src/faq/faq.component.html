Frequently Asked Questions

Linking your PlayStation account means giving Librarian a token called an NPSSO. That's a reasonable thing to be cautious about, so here's exactly what happens when you do it.

What is an NPSSO token?

It's a session token Sony issues after you sign in at playstation.com. It works like a password — anyone who has it can act as you on PlayStation Network until it expires (about 60 days). Treat it the same way you'd treat a password: don't paste it into a site you don't trust, and don't share it.

How do I get mine?

Sign in at playstation.com, then in that same browser (not a different one, not incognito) visit https://ca.account.sony.com/api/v1/ssocookie. The page returns a short block of JSON with an npsso field — that value is your token. Paste it into the PSN link form on your PSN settings page.

What does Librarian do with it?

Librarian passes your NPSSO to Curator, the API that actually talks to PlayStation. Curator uses it once, immediately, to ask Sony for an access token and a refresh token — then discards the NPSSO. It is never written to a database and never appears in a log line anywhere in Curator's code.

Why does linking need my email at all?

Before Curator keeps anything, it checks that the email PlayStation has on file for your account — and confirms is verified — matches the email your sign-in already established. That comparison happens in memory and is never written down; there is no email column anywhere in Curator's database. If the emails don't match, or PSN reports the email as unverified, the tokens Curator just obtained are deleted immediately and nothing is linked.

What happens to the access and refresh tokens?

They're encrypted before they're stored, and only Curator holds the key. You can see when they expire on your PSN settings page.

How do I revoke access?

Two options, and you can use either or both. Unlink on your PSN settings page deletes Curator's copy of your tokens right away. Sony doesn't give third parties a way to revoke a token remotely, so if you want to be certain the NPSSO itself can't be used by anyone, sign out of all devices at playstation.com — that invalidates it at the source.

Can I see everything Curator has done with my account?

Yes. Every linking attempt, unlink, library refresh, and trophy fetch Curator performs on your behalf is recorded in an action log you can view and download yourself — see the privacy policy for what's in it and how long it's kept.

What are the RAWG/OpenCritic keys on my PSN settings page for?

They're entirely optional. Curator can enrich your library with genre and critic-score data from RAWG and OpenCritic, but it doesn't provision a shared key for every user — that wouldn't scale. If you want that enrichment, you can provide your own free RAWG and/or OpenCritic key. If you skip this, your library still works — you just won't get that extra metadata for titles nobody's key has resolved yet.

How does Curator know my key is valid?

When you save a key, Curator makes one small request to that provider to confirm it's accepted before storing anything. If the provider rejects it, you'll see that immediately and the key is never saved — so you always have a chance to correct it, instead of finding out only when a later library refresh fails.

Is my RAWG/OpenCritic key ever shown to me again, or logged anywhere?

No. It's encrypted the moment you save it, the same way your PlayStation tokens are. Your PSN settings page only ever shows whether a key is configured and when it was added — never the value, before or after saving.

What happens to the data my key retrieves?

Only the game metadata it retrieves (title, genre, critic score) — never your key. That metadata is added to Curator's shared game catalog so future lookups for that same game, by any user, don't need to call RAWG/OpenCritic again. This is why some of your games may already show enrichment data even if you've never added a key — someone else's key, or Curator's own periodic catalog refresh, already resolved it.

How do I remove a RAWG/OpenCritic key?

From your PSN settings page — each key has its own "Remove" button, independent of the other. Removing one doesn't affect the other or anything else in your account.

Why doesn't my whole library get enriched right away?

RAWG is fast, but OpenCritic's free tier is limited, so OpenCritic data for a very large or brand-new library fills in gradually across a few refreshes rather than all at once — contributed cooperatively, a bit at a time, by everyone's refreshes. Your library page shows a RAWG and OpenCritic rating per game, with a dash for whichever hasn't resolved yet, so you can always see exactly what's been resolved so far.

What's on my public profile?

Your profile is private until you turn it on yourself, on your profile settings page. Once public, it can show your PSN account id (or "Unlinked user" if you haven't linked one), your library, your saved collections, your trophy summary, and your PSN online id — each one is its own toggle, and each one is off by default. Your follower and following lists are always visible to anyone, public profile or not, since that's Librarian's own data and has nothing to do with PlayStation. Your region is never shown anywhere in Librarian, on your own profile or anyone else's.

How does Librarian show someone else's trophies without their password?

It doesn't need their password, or their PSN session at all. When you view someone's public profile and they've chosen to show their trophies, Librarian looks them up using your own linked PlayStation account — the same way looking up any other player's public trophies works on PSN itself. Nothing of theirs is ever touched. If you haven't linked your own PSN account, or your link has expired, that section just doesn't show — you won't see an error, and nobody is notified.

How does follow/unfollow work?

You can follow anyone, whether or not their profile is public — following doesn't require the other person to show you anything. Follower and following lists are always visible on every profile, so you can always see who follows someone and who they follow. Following someone you already follow, or unfollowing someone you don't, is harmless — it just does nothing.

Is this actually safe, or am I just supposed to trust you?

Don't take our word for it — both apps are open source. Librarian (this site, including the BFF that proxies your requests) is at github.com/crgolden/Librarian, and Curator (the API that handles your NPSSO, verifies your email, and stores your tokens) is at github.com/crgolden/Curator. Every claim on this page and in the privacy policy is something you can go read in the code.
